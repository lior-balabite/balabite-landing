# BalaBite Logo Design Specification
### The Dagesh Bet
### March 19, 2026

---

## The Concept

The BalaBite logo is a geometrically refined **Paleo-Hebrew letter "bet"** with a **dagesh dot** at its center.

- **Bet** = the ancient Hebrew letter meaning "house" (bayit). The oldest known symbol for "house" in Western written language, dating back ~4,000 years.
- **Dagesh** = the dot placed inside a Hebrew bet to give it the "B" sound — as in **B**alaBite.
- **Together** = the master inside the house. Ba'al HaBayit. The owner at the center of their domain.

The logo is not a clever design trick. It is the most direct possible symbol: the ancient letter for "house" with the owner at its center. That directness is rare and powerful.

---

## The Story

> "The oldest symbol for 'house' in Western civilization, with a dot at its center representing the owner — the Ba'al HaBayit — who makes it a home."

- The letter bet (ב) is the first letter of the Torah ("Bereshit" — in the beginning)
- In rabbinical tradition, bet is shaped like a house with one open wall — open to the future
- The Paleo-Hebrew form (~1000-600 BCE) is visually distinct from any modern letter
- The dagesh dot represents emphasis, strength, intentionality — and the owner within

## Form Description

### Shape
A geometrically refined Paleo-Hebrew bet oriented with its **opening at the bottom**. The form resembles a smooth, continuous arch:
- Begins at the lower-left
- Sweeps up and over to the right
- Curves back down — creating an asymmetric enclosure
- The opening at the bottom = the doorway. The house is always open.

A small, perfectly round **dagesh dot** sits centered inside the enclosure, slightly above center — the hearth, the focal point, the owner within.

### Stroke Character
- **Monoline** with very subtle taper (slightly thicker at the base, slightly thinner at the apex)
- **Rounded terminals** — warm, inviting, not sharp
- **Single confident weight** — no thin/thick calligraphic contrast
- The form derives from the calligraphic original but has been distilled to geometric precision
- It should feel ancient AND like it was designed yesterday

### Proportions
- The opening at the bottom is a doorway, not a wide gap — the arms come close enough to define an interior space
- The dagesh dot is approximately 15-18% of the mark's overall width
- Generous negative space inside the enclosure — the interior should feel like a room, not a slot

## Color System

The mark is defined in **black first**. Every great mark works in black on white and white on black.

### Primary Applications

| Context | Mark Color | Background | Notes |
|---------|-----------|------------|-------|
| Standard / digital | Black | White/cream | Default usage |
| Reversed | Cream (#FAF5EE) | Charcoal (#1C1410) | App icon, dark contexts |
| Brand moments | Wine-red (#722F37) | Cream (#FAF5EE) | Hero sections, brand materials |
| Premium / physical | Aged gold (#C4956A) | Dark surfaces | Embossing, foil stamp, menus |
| On warm surfaces | Wine-red (#722F37) | Natural material | Print, packaging, stationery |

### Color Notes
- Wine-red (#722F37): not bright red, not burgundy-brown. The color of a Barolo held up to candlelight.
- Aged gold (#C4956A): warm, dark, aged. The gold of a menorah, not a trophy.
- Charcoal (#1C1410): near-black with warmth. Not pure black, not gray.

## Wordmark

### Lockup
- The bet mark on the left, "BalaBite" on the right
- ".ai" can be integrated or slightly de-emphasized (lighter weight or smaller size)

### Typography
- Clean humanist sans-serif at medium weight
- Slightly warm — not geometric (too cold) or grotesque (too generic)
- The **B's in "BalaBite" share the same stroke weight and curve radius** as the bet mark — they are cousins, not copies
- The **dot on the "i"** can optionally be rendered as a dagesh circle, creating a typographic link to the mark
- Generous letter-spacing

### Hierarchy
- The mark does the heavy lifting for personality
- The wordmark is clear, legible, and does not compete

## Scale and Minimum Size

| Application | Minimum Size | Notes |
|-------------|-------------|-------|
| Digital (screen) | 32px | Dagesh dot remains legible |
| Physical (print) | 12mm | Clean reproduction |
| Favicon | 16px | Mark only, no wordmark |
| App icon | 60x60px visible | Mark in wine-red or gold on dark charcoal |

### At Small Scale
- The mark + dagesh must remain legible
- Below minimum size, consider mark without dagesh as a fallback

### At Large Scale
- The subtle taper and rounded terminals become visible and satisfying
- The interior space feels architectural — like looking into a room

## Application Specs

### App Icon
- Dark charcoal background (#1C1410)
- Bet mark in aged gold (#C4956A) or wine-red (#722F37)
- Generous padding — the mark floats with authority
- No wordmark

### Website Navigation
- Black mark on light cream background
- Paired with "BalaBite" wordmark
- Compact lockup

### Website Hero
- Large bet mark in wine-red (#722F37) as a textural, almost architectural element
- Can be used as a background watermark at low opacity

### Physical / Print
- Wine-red on natural paper, cream card stock
- Foil-stamped in aged gold on dark materials
- Embossed / debossed on leather, menus, packaging

### Wax Seal (brand collateral, not on characters)
- Mark centered in a plain circle, no text around perimeter
- Generous margin to seal edge
- Wine-red wax
- The mark is recessed in the stamp (intaglio) so it appears raised in the wax impression
- The raised bet catches light on its edges — the dagesh dot appears as a small jewel-like raised circle

## Design System Extensions

The dagesh dot can be extracted as an independent design element:
- Bullet points in brand materials
- Loading indicator / cursor
- Notification dot
- The "i" dots in the wordmark

The open-bottom enclosure shape can be used as:
- A framing device for photography or content cards
- A section divider
- An architectural motif in UI

---

## What the Mark Is NOT

- It is NOT a generic arch or doorway
- It is NOT a Latin letter (d, P, 9, etc.) — the orientation prevents this
- It is NOT calligraphy or hand-drawn — it is geometrically precise
- It is NOT decorative — every element has meaning
- It does NOT appear on the origami hat characters (they are identified by their eyes and paper texture)
